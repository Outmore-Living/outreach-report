What Works (Proven Patterns)
  1. Personal geographic connection -- Georgia sequence led with "I grew up in Marietta" and hit 21% reply rate. The UT Austin connection sequence hit 28% reply rate. Personal ties are the single strongest predictor of engagement.
  2. Industry-specific framing -- Wineries sequence painted a picture ("Imagine your outdoor spaces full year-round -- without propane heaters") rather than listing features. 12% reply rate.
  3. Conversational tone -- Short sentences, no jargon, no bullet lists. Winners read like a person talking, not marketing collateral.
  4. Direct CTA with time specificity -- "I'd love to connect for 15 minutes" outperformed every soft CTA like "Would you be open to seeing our catalog?"
  5. Single idea per email -- Georgia Step 1 has one idea: local connection + invite to trade program. Three sentences. The hospitality sequence crammed seven ideas into one email and got 2% reply rate.
  6. Re: threading in subject lines -- Steps 2-3 using "Re:" consistently maintained open rates within 5-10 points of Step 1.
  7. Company name in subject -- Subjects with {{company_name}} opened 5-15% higher than generic subjects.
What Fails (Anti-Patterns to Eliminate)
  1. Bullet-list feature dumps -- "Hotels love working with us because: (bullet) (bullet) (bullet)" reads like a brochure. Every underperformer uses this pattern.
  2. Commission/revenue angle for designers -- "Earning them over $7,500 in commission" is off-putting. Designers care about design quality, not commission checks.
  3. Copy recycling -- Steps 5-8 are identical across 8+ sequences. The Retirement Communities sequence references "wineries" in Step 6. Copy-paste errors destroy credibility.
  4. "Should I stay or should I go?" -- Used as Step 8 subject across every sequence, consistently producing the lowest open rates (15-28%). Exhausted.
  5. "Fallen and can't get up" joke -- Used in every breakup email. Risks undermining luxury brand credibility. Especially problematic when sent to retirement communities.
  6. Soft hedging CTAs -- "Curious? I'd love to send you a catalog or schedule a quick intro call" / "If this is something interesting to you, I'd be glad to share." Creates no urgency, gives easy opt-out.
  7. Company-first openers -- "I'm Kevin, CEO of Outmore Living..." used in 14 of 18 active sequences. Self-centered opening. The Georgia sequence (the only winner) opens with the recipient.
  8. 8-9 step sequences -- Steps 6-9 produce <5% of total replies while adding deliverability risk. Dead weight.

Rules for the AI generation prompt:

  1. Include {{company_name}} or {{first_name}} in Step 1 subjects (5-15% open lift proven)

Copy Principles (Encode into Prompt Templates)

Update /prompts/message-generation.md to enforce these rules:

1. Open with the recipient, not yourself
First sentence must reference the prospect, their company, their city, or something specific to them. Never start with "I'm Kevin" or "My name is."
2. One idea per email, maximum
Step 1: personal connection + invite. Step 2: a different angle or social proof. Step 3: direct ask. Step 4: farewell. No email should contain more than one value proposition.
3. No bullet lists in cold emails
All copy must be conversational prose. Bullets signal marketing collateral and immediately reduce perceived authenticity.
4. Direct CTAs with specificity
Every email must end with a concrete next step: "15 minutes this week?", "Can I send a 2-minute video?", "Want me to send a swatch kit?" Never "Let me know if interested" or "I'd be happy to share more."
5. Paint a picture, don't list features
Instead of "5 temperature levels, 10+ hours cordless, FSC-certified teak" write "Picture your guests staying outside past sunset in November -- no propane heaters, no wires, just warm teak furniture they don't want to leave."
6. Never recycle copy across segments
Every sequence must have segment-specific language. A winery GM and a retirement community director have fundamentally different motivations.

4. RLHF Feedback Loop Integration

4.1 Existing RLHF Mechanisms in the Codebase

The OUTMORE Outreach system already has a comprehensive RLHF architecture. Here is what exists and how each component feeds the learning loop.

Voice Training System (3 Modes)

Location: src/app/dashboard/training/, src/components/training/, src/app/api/training/

ModeComponentWeightSignal
A/B Pickab-pick.tsx60% of challengesBinary preference signal -- which style the sender prefers
Rate & Reviserate-revise.tsx25% of challengesQuality signal (1-5 stars) + rewrite signal (gold-standard text)
Rankrank-options.tsx15% of challengesOrdinal preference across 4 options -- reveals gradient, not just binary

Training responses are stored in voice_training_responses table. On session completion, the system triggers preference aggregation via the training-aggregation.md prompt, which extracts structured preferences into voice_preferences with confidence scores.

Current coverage: 10 preference categories (cold_open, follow_up, reply, objection, meeting_ask, breakup, subject_line, tone, length, cta) with confidence scoring from 0.3 to 1.0.

Voice Profile Generation

Location: src/app/api/jobs/voice-rebuild/route.ts, prompts/voice-profile-generation.md

The voice profile is the central artifact that drives all downstream email generation. It is rebuilt when:

  • 25+ new emails are ingested from Gmail
  • Manually triggered from the dashboard
  • (Future) When drift is detected

Inputs consumed:

  1. Top 100 voice_emails ranked by quality_score (real sent emails)
  2. Starred emails (booked_meeting = true) -- weighted heavily
  3. Voice preferences from training sessions
  4. Training response signals (last 50 responses)
  5. Performance metrics from Instantly (last 4 weeks of open/click/reply/bounce/meeting rates by angle, persona, segment)

Output: Structured JSON with voice_summary (narrative brief) and style_guide (formality, length, greeting, CTA, tone, humor, personalization approach, vocabulary, structural patterns).

Key insight: The voice profile generation prompt already instructs Opus to "incorporate performance data -- if certain angles or segments have notably higher open/click/reply rates, note which voice patterns correlate with those wins." This means objective market feedback already flows into subjective voice generation.
Quality Scoring (Generation-Time Gate)

Location: src/app/api/jobs/sequence-generate/route.ts, prompts/quality-scoring.md

Every generated sequence goes through a quality scoring step before being presented for human review:

  1. Messages are scored 0-1 across 5 dimensions: Voice Match (25%), Personalization (25%), Compelling Hook (20%), CTA Clarity (15%), Technical Quality (15%)
  2. If any message scores below 0.7, it is regenerated with the quality feedback appended to the prompt
  3. Maximum 2 retries before proceeding with best available

Current thresholds: MIN_QUALITY_SCORE = 0.7, MAX_RETRIES = 2

Approve/Reject Signals from Dashboard

Location: src/app/api/sequences/[id]/approve/route.ts, src/app/api/sequences/[id]/reject/route.ts

When a sender reviews a sequence in the dashboard:

  • Approve sets sequence status to approved, messages to approved
  • Reject stores rejection_feedback text on the sequence -- the sender explains what was wrong
Current gap: Rejection feedback is stored but not yet systematically fed back into the generation pipeline. This is the most critical missing link.
Weekly Learning Analysis

Location: src/app/api/jobs/weekly-learning/route.ts, prompts/weekly-learning.md

Runs every Monday at 3:00 AM UTC. Performs:

  1. Pulls last 30 days of message_performance data
  2. Aggregates by 4 dimensions: angle, persona, segment, personalization_tier
  3. Calculates rates: open, reply, positive_reply, meeting, bounce, click
  4. Stores aggregated stats in performance_insights table
  5. Calls Claude Opus for strategic analysis
  6. Auto-adds positive-reply emails to voice_emails as source = 'system_winner'
This is the critical reinforcement loop: Winning emails (positive replies) automatically become part of the voice profile training corpus. Over time, the voice profile biases toward patterns that actually work in market.
Reply Classification & Instantly Webhook Pipeline

Location: src/app/api/webhooks/instantly/route.ts, prompts/reply-classification.md, prompts/reply-draft.md

Every reply webhook from Instantly triggers:

  1. Reply sentiment classification (Sonnet)
  2. Performance tracking update (message_performance table)
  3. Auto-reply pipeline (if confidence > 0.85 and sentiment is in allowed list)
  4. HubSpot write-back for positive replies

Data captured per reply: sentiment, confidence, summary, suggested_response, urgency, action_required

The Instantly webhook captures 6 event types that feed performance tracking:

  • email_sent -- delivery confirmation
  • email_opened -- engagement signal
  • reply_received -- primary outcome signal (triggers classification)
  • email_bounced -- data quality signal (marks contact as do_not_contact)
  • lead_unsubscribed -- compliance signal
  • link_clicked -- engagement signal

4.3 Missing Links and Integration Improvements

Gap 1: Rejection Feedback Not Feeding Back Into Generation
Current state: When a sender rejects a sequence, the rejection_feedback text is stored on the sequences table but never consumed by the generation pipeline.

Fix: Add a "rejection patterns" input to the message generation prompt.

Implementation

  1. In /api/jobs/sequence-generate/route.ts, before generating a new sequence, query recent rejections for this sender
  2. Format as a "What to avoid" section in the message generation prompt
  3. Add this as a new variable {rejection_patterns} in prompts/message-generation.md
## Recent Rejection Feedback (avoid these patterns) - "Too formal, I'd never say 'I would be delighted'" (rejected 2 days ago, angle: design_upgrade) - "Way too long, needs to be half this length" (rejected 3 days ago, angle: seasonal_revenue)
Priority: HIGH -- this is the fastest way to improve generation quality. Each rejection is a direct signal from the sender about what is wrong.
Gap 2: Step-Level Performance Not Tracked Granularly
Current state: message_performance tracks which message got opened/replied/clicked, and the weekly learning aggregates by angle/persona/segment/tier. But there is no aggregation by step number or sequence structure.

Fix: Add step_number to the message_performance table and include step-level analysis in the weekly learning prompt.

Implementation

  1. Add step_number int column to message_performance
  2. Populate it from messages.step_number when creating performance records
  3. Add "step" as a 5th dimension in the weekly learning aggregation
  4. Update prompts/weekly-learning.md to include step-level funnel analysis
Priority: HIGH -- this data directly informs the optimal sequence length and step structure.
Gap 3: Subject Line A/B Testing Not Instrumented
Current state: The system generates one subject per step. There is no mechanism for testing subject line variants against each other.

Fix: Generate 2 subject line variants per step and track which performs better.

Implementation

  1. Update prompts/message-generation.md to generate 2 subject variants per message
  2. Add variant column to messages table (already exists, currently unused -- always 'a')
  3. In sync-instantly, alternate variants across leads: even contacts get variant A, odd get variant B
  4. Track open rates by variant in message_performance
  5. After 50+ sends per variant, auto-promote the winner in the weekly learning cycle
Priority: MEDIUM -- subject lines are the first gate. A 5% open rate improvement cascades through the entire funnel.
Gap 4: Voice Drift Detection Not Implemented
Current state: The SPEC describes a voice drift detection mechanism (Appendix) but it is not built. The drift_score and last_drift_check columns exist on voice_profiles but are never populated.

Fix: Implement the monthly drift check as a cron job.

Implementation

  1. Create /api/jobs/voice-drift/route.ts
  2. Pull 10 recent AI-generated emails + 10 recent real emails per sender
  3. Send both sets to Claude (Opus) without labels
  4. Claude rates each 0-1 on authenticity against the voice profile
  5. If AI email average < 0.7, flag for profile regeneration
  6. Store drift_score and last_drift_check on voice_profiles
  7. Add to weekly Slack notification
Priority: MEDIUM -- becomes critical as the system generates more emails and the voice profile could gradually diverge from the sender's actual evolving style.
Gap 5: Persona-Level Performance Feedback Not Closing the Loop
Current state: The persona_voice_modifiers table stores per-persona adjustments (tone, formality, CTA style, vocabulary). The weekly learning analyzes by persona. But there is no automated mechanism to update persona modifiers based on performance data.

Fix: Add persona modifier adjustment to the weekly learning pipeline.

Implementation

  1. In the weekly learning job, after Opus generates the strategic analysis, extract persona-specific recommendations
  2. For personas with 50+ sends and clear performance signals, auto-generate updated modifier suggestions
  3. Present these as "Suggested persona adjustments" in the dashboard, requiring human approval before applying
  4. Track modifier versions over time to measure impact
Priority: LOW -- requires sufficient per-persona data volume. Focus on this after 2-3 months of operation.
Gap 6: Angle Performance Not Feeding Back into Angle Selection
Current state: The angle selection prompt (prompts/angle-selection.md) presents 8 angles but does not include performance data about which angles work for which segments.

Fix: Include angle performance data in the angle selection prompt.

Implementation

  1. In sequence-generate/route.ts, query performance_insights for angle performance
  2. Add as a new section in the angle selection prompt
## Historical Angle Performance - seasonal_revenue: 52% open, 4.2% reply, 1.1% positive (n=340) - guest_experience: 61% open, 6.8% reply, 2.3% positive (n=220) - design_upgrade: 48% open, 3.1% reply, 0.8% positive (n=180) Prefer angles with higher positive_reply_rate when the prospect fits.
Priority: HIGH -- this is the most direct way to bias the system toward angles that actually convert.

4.5 How Approve/Reject Signals Improve Quality Over Time

Immediate Effect

Rejected sequences store feedback text. If the sender explains "too long" or "wrong tone," the next regeneration attempt includes this feedback.

Short-Term Effect

Before generating new sequences, the system queries recent rejections. Common patterns (e.g., "bullet lists" in 5 of 10 rejections) are explicitly listed as things to avoid. Quality scoring threshold can be dynamically adjusted -- if rejection rate >30%, raise MIN_QUALITY_SCORE from 0.7 to 0.8.

Medium-Term Effect

Weekly learning analysis includes approval/rejection ratios by angle, persona, and segment. Opus identifies systematic quality issues ("Sequences using operational_ease angle are rejected 60% of the time -- recommend deprioritizing"). Insights are stored and fed into the next voice profile rebuild.

Long-Term Effect

Training sessions present challenges informed by real performance data and rejection patterns. Voice profile rebuilds incorporate both training signals AND objective performance data. Over 3-6 months, the profile converges on the sender's authentic voice AND what works in market.

Measurement: Track these metrics weekly to validate the loop is working:
  • Approval rate (target: >80% after 4 weeks of operation)
  • Average quality score of approved sequences (target: trending upward)
  • Rejection reason distribution (target: fewer repeated reasons over time)
  • Reply rate of approved sequences vs. old Amplemarket sequences (target: >2x improvement)
