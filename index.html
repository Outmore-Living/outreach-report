Across all sequences, Outmore sent multi-step cold email campaigns to 11,768 unique contacts spanning interior designers, hospitality executives, golf clubs, wineries, custom home builders, retirement communities, resort operators, and country clubs. The overall reply rate was 3.4%, with the majority being negative responses: "not interested," "hard no," or asking to be removed.

Only 94 contacts (0.8%) were flagged as "Interested" — a thin pipeline from a large volume of outreach. The data reveals clear patterns in what copy, targeting, and structure actually drives engagement vs. what falls flat.

Step 1 Email Copy: Top vs Bottom (Side by Side)

Winner: RID Georgia (21% reply rate)

Hi {{first_name}}, My name is Kevin, CEO of Outmore Living, an Austin-based outdoor furniture company that builds heated outdoor furniture. Noticed you were in Georgia...I grew up in Marietta, just outside of Atlanta! We have been getting a lot of demand from customers in Georgia, so I thought it would be great to connect with experienced interior designers like yourself both to personally invite you to join our trade program and to see how we could best service you and your clients. If you think our product would be a good fit for your clients, I'd love to connect for 15 minutes! Thanks! Kevin

Loser: Interior Designer Outreach (1% reply rate)

Hi {{first_name}}, Could your clients use their outdoor spaces year-round? I'm the CEO of Outmore Living, an Austin-based company that builds heated outdoor furniture. We help designers create unique spaces for clients and offer a 20% trade program discount with free fabric customization. We recently helped a designer in Michigan build a custom outdoor space for a client, earning them over $7,500 in commission. Would you be open to seeing our catalog? Thanks, Kevin
Why RID Georgia Won

Personal hook: "I grew up in Marietta, just outside of Atlanta" immediately establishes a human connection. It signals this is not a mass email.

Specificity: "getting a lot of demand from customers in Georgia" makes the recipient feel like part of a local movement, not a cold list.

Direct CTA: "I'd love to connect for 15 minutes" is concrete and time-bounded. Compare to the loser's "Would you be open to seeing our catalog?" which asks for nothing specific.

Tone: Casual and conversational. No bullet lists. No feature dumps. Reads like one person talking to another.

Why Interior Designer Outreach Failed

Opens with a question nobody asked: "Could your clients use their outdoor spaces year-round?" feels like a telemarketing script.

Feature dump: Jumps straight to "20% trade program discount with free fabric customization" -- selling before earning attention.

Commission angle is off-putting: "earning them over $7,500 in commission" sounds manipulative. Designers don't want to be sold on commission; they want to be sold on design quality.

Weak CTA: "Would you be open to seeing our catalog?" is passive and low-commitment, yet somehow still feels pushy in context.

4. Email Copy Analysis

1. Personalization: The #1 Differentiator

The single strongest predictor of reply rate was personalization in the opening line.

"Noticed you were in Georgia...I grew up in Marietta, just outside of Atlanta!" -- RID Georgia, Step 1 (21% reply rate)

No other sequence has anything close to this level of personal specificity. The hospitality and designer sequences all use the same template opening:

"I'm Kevin, co-founder/CEO/founder of Outmore Living..." -- used in 14 of 18 active sequences

The Georgia sequence is the only one that leads with a human connection before introducing the company. Every other sequence leads with the company.

2. Tone: Conversational vs. Brochure

Winners are conversational. The Georgia and Wineries sequences read like someone actually talking. Short sentences. No jargon. No buzzwords.

Losers read like marketing collateral. The Luxury Hospitality Executives and newer designer sequences are packed with bullet lists and formal language:

"Hotels love working with us because our heated furniture: - Enhances comfort without compromising aesthetics - Increases F&B revenue while decreasing operation expenses - Wows their guests while delivering unparalleled outdoor comfort" -- Luxury Hospitality Executives, Step 1 (2% reply rate)

Compare to the winery version of the same pitch:

"Imagine if the outdoor spaces you design could be used year-round -- without propane heaters, wires, or bulky fixtures." -- Wineries, Step 1 (12% reply rate)

Same product. Same sender. Completely different response rates. The winery version paints a picture; the hospitality version lists features.

3. CTAs: Direct vs. Soft

Winners ask for something specific:

"If you think our product would be a good fit for your clients, I'd love to connect for 15 minutes!" -- RID Georgia
"Would you be open to a quick call to see if we'd be a good partner fit?" -- Resorts (7% reply)

Losers hedge:

"Curious? I'd love to send you a catalog or schedule a quick intro call." -- Luxury Designers 01.04.26 (1% reply)
"If this is something interesting to you, I'd be glad to share a few examples or pricing details." -- Hospitality Executives (2% reply)

The soft CTAs ("I'd be glad to share if interested") create no urgency and give the recipient an easy out. The direct CTAs ("15 minutes" / "quick call") set a concrete expectation.

4. Length: Not About Word Count

The top and bottom performers have similar word counts (80-120 words for Step 1). The difference is not length but density of ideas.

Winners: one idea per email. The Georgia Step 1 has exactly one idea: "I'm local, we're getting demand in Georgia, let's connect." Three sentences, one thread.

Losers: multiple value props crammed in. The Hospitality Executives Step 1 tries to communicate: (1) we make heated furniture, (2) it uses patented technology, (3) hotels love us, (4) it enhances comfort, (5) it increases F&B revenue, (6) it reduces propane costs, (7) guests are wowed. That is seven ideas in one email.

5. Copy Recycling: The Same Emails Across 6+ Sequences

Steps 5-8 are virtually identical across Wineries, Custom Home Builders, Golf Clubs, Designer campaigns, and Retirement Communities. Examples of recycled blocks:

Step 5 (used in 8+ sequences):

"Guessing you are busy dealing with demanding clients, but I wanted to make sure you saw my last email."

Step 7 (used in 8+ sequences):

"I do my best to avoid wasting anyone's time. If I've got the wrong person, I'd love to stop wasting yours."

Step 8 (used in 8+ sequences):

"I've tried to reach you a few times... which tells me one of three things: (1) You're all set and I should stop bothering you. (2) You're still interested but haven't had the time. (3) You've fallen and can't get up..."

The "fallen and can't get up" joke is used in every single sequence's breakup email. It was likely charming the first time and tired by the tenth. For a luxury brand, this kind of humor risks undermining brand credibility.

The Retirement Communities sequence still references "wineries" in its Step 6 email body: "When I talk to wineries, many of them want to create outdoor spaces..." -- a clear copy-paste error that signals zero personalization to recipients.

6. Subject Line Analysis

Key Patterns

Re: threading drives opens

Steps 2-3 use "Re:" threading (replying to the original subject) and consistently show open rates within 5-10 points of Step 1. The "Re:" prefix signals an ongoing conversation and bypasses the "cold email" mental filter.

Example: "Re: {{company_name}} / Outmore Living Partnership?" maintains 46% open rate at Step 2 in the Georgia sequence.

"Should I stay or should I go?" underperforms

Used as the Step 8 subject across nearly all sequences, this subject line consistently produces the lowest open rates in each sequence:

"Should I stay or should I go?" -- Designer 05.0515%
"Should I stay or should I go?" -- Custom Home Builders23%
"Should I stay or should I go?" -- Wineries25%
"Should I stay or should I go?" -- Golf Clubs26%
"Should I stay or should I go?" -- Retirement Communities28%

By Step 8, recipients have likely already decided to ignore the thread. A Clash reference does not change that.

Company name in subject correlates with higher opens

Subjects that include {{company_name}} or the recipient's company consistently open 5-15% higher than generic subjects:

"Enhancing {{company_name}}'s Outdoor Experience" -- NJ Resorts33%

9. Recommendations

  1. Personalize the first line with geography or a company-specific observation.

    The Georgia sequence proved that a single personal detail ("I grew up in Marietta") can 10x reply rates. Before sending any sequence, research the recipient's city, a recent project, or a visible detail about their property/company. This takes 30 seconds per contact and produces dramatically better results than blasting 2,000 people with the same template.

    Benchmark: Georgia at 21% vs. generic sequences at 1-2%.

  2. One idea per email. Kill the bullet lists.

    Every underperforming sequence crams 3-7 value propositions into a single email with bullet-point formatting. This reads like a brochure, not a conversation. The best performers (Georgia, Wineries) present one idea, clearly and conversationally. Lead with curiosity or a scenario, not a feature list.

    Before: "Hotels love working with us because: (bullet) (bullet) (bullet)"

    After: "Picture your guests staying outside past sunset in November, no propane heaters in sight."

  3. Direct CTAs with a specific time ask.

    Replace soft CTAs like "I'd be glad to share if interested" and "Curious? I'd love to send a catalog" with concrete asks: "Do you have 15 minutes Thursday?" or "Can I send a 2-minute video walkthrough?" The Georgia sequence's "I'd love to connect for 15 minutes" outperformed every other CTA in the dataset.

  4. Stop recycling copy across sequences.

    Steps 5-8 are identical across 8+ sequences targeting completely different audiences. Winery operators, retirement community managers, and interior designers have different motivations, pain points, and decision criteria. The Retirement Communities sequence literally references "wineries" in its Step 6 -- a copy-paste error that undermines credibility. Write audience-specific copy for every step, or cut to fewer steps.

  5. Cut sequences to 4-5 steps maximum.

    Steps 6-9 produce less than 5% of total replies combined while adding sending volume that increases deliverability risk. A tighter sequence of 4-5 emails with a strong breakup email at the end will capture 95% of reply value. The extra steps are not earning their keep -- they are just burning sender reputation.

    Proposed structure: (1) Personal intro + hook, (2) Social proof/scenario, (3) Direct ask, (4) Value add, (5) Clean breakup.

The bottom line: This is not a volume problem. Outmore reached nearly 12,000 contacts. It is a quality problem. The one sequence with genuine personalization (Georgia) produced 10-20x the response of everything else. The path forward is not more emails to more people -- it is better emails to the right people.
